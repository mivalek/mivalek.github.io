Sampling Distribution

In research we make claims about the world in general. To be able to do this we take samples of the world and analyse them with statistics. The problem with samples is that, because they are only a small section of the population, we cannot be sure that our sample accurately represents the population. To be able to understand the uncertainty in the relationship between our sample and the population we use inferential statistics.

When it comes to making inferences from samples to populations, one concept in particular is really important to understand: the sampling distribution. Let’s illustrate this concept on the example of a game of Scrabble. In Scrabble, you form words using letter tiles and score points for the letters you use. Each letter has a point value, represented by the number at the bottom of the tile. The values are based on how common the letter is in the language and how difficult it is to use in a word.

The letters have the following values;

  • 1 Point: A, E, I, L, N, O, R, S, T and U.
  • 2 Points: D and G.
  • 3 Points: B, C, M and P.
  • 4 Points: F, H, V, W and Y.
  • 5 Points: K.
  • 8 Points: J and X.
  • 10 Points: Q and Z

The mean value of a full set of 100 tiles is 1.87. The 100 tiles make up the population that we are sampling from and so 1.87 is the population mean (\(\mu\)).

At the beginning of a game, each player picks 7 tiles from the bag. Each of these picks is a random sample. The number on the tile is its value. For each sample, we can calculate the sample mean: the average value of the 7 tiles.

For example, if you picked the tiles T, A, D, I, N, M, O with the values of 1, 1, 2, 1, 1, 3, 1, the mean of these tiles is 1.42.

If you were to put back those 7 tiles and replace them with another set of 7 tiles - for instance, I, Z, C, E, E, B, B with values of 1, 10, 3, 1, 1, 3, 3 - the sample mean would be different, 3.14. This is because you will have picked a different set of tiles with different values. Sometimes you are unlucky and pick only the difficult letters, sometimes you only have the frequent letters and other times it’s a mix. This variability between samples is called sampling variation.

This will happen each time we replace and take out another 7 tiles as the value of the sample tiles changes, the mean value of the draw will be slightly different.

If you could pick all possible combinations of 7 tiles from the bag and write down the mean value of each one, you would end up with a lot of numbers, each representing the mean of one of the samples. These numbers would have their own distribution: there would be some small values, there would be some relatively large ones, but mostly, they would be close to the population mean (the mean value of all 100 tiles). This distribution of all the means of all the samples of the same size (in our case 7) is known as the sampling distribution of the mean. If, on the other hand, we were looking at the median value, instead of the mean value of the tiles, the distribution of all the medians would be the sampling distribution of the median. Each statistic (e.g., mean, median, standard deviation) has its own sampling distribution that is different in every population and for every possible sample size.

The sampling distribution has a few important properties:

  1. Its mean is equal to the population value of the calculated statistic; In our example, where we calculated the average value of the tiles, the mean of the sampling distribution is the same as the average value of all the tiles in the population, 1.87. If we were calculating, for example, the median, the mean of the sampling distribution of the median would be the same as the population median.
  2. The shape of the sampling distribution of the mean gets more and more normal (bell-shaped and symmetrical) as the size of the samples that we pick increases. This is true no matter the shape of the distribution of the population. This property however, applies only to certain statistics such as the mean, and not to others like the standard deviation.
  3. Its standard deviation, which is a measure of how much the individual value of the statistic (e.g., the average tile value) changes from sample to sample is called the standard error. The standard error (SE) is proportional to the variability in the population and the size of the samples drawn from the population. The more variability in a population, the larger the SE. If the population varies a lot there is a high chance that the sample we randomly pick could be wildly different from the population mean. However, the larger the size of the sample we’re choosing is, the smaller the SE gets. That’s because larger samples are less likely to produce means that are wildly different from the population mean. In a way, the standard error measures the uncertainty of the relationship between the sample statistic and its population.

Sampling distribution is very useful because we don’t have to pick all the possible sample tiles from the scrabble bag to know the characteristics (mean, approximate shape and spread) of the sampling distribution. We can even estimate the standard error (SE) just from the standard deviation (SD) of a single sample and the size (N) of the sample as \(\frac{SD}{\sqrt{N}}\).
