The \(\chi^2\)-test

Modelling categorical data

  • We talked about how the \(\chi^2\)-distribution is useful for assessing model fit.
  • When working with categorical data, we can model their distributions
  • \(\chi^2\)-test allows us to see how our model distribution fits the data
  • By extension, we can model relationships between 2 categorical variables

Goodness-of-fit test

  • Let’s look at the representation of MSc study programmes in the first USMR lecture
  • Out of 148 attendees, there were 41 CogSci, 52 LEL, and 55 Psych students
  • Say we’re expecting equal representation of the three programmes – this is our \(H_0\)
  • Is the split above due to random statistical fluctuations or is there a systematic difference between our expectation and observation?

Goodness-of-fit test

  • We do this using the sum of scaled squares (sum of squared differences over expected frequencies)
  • \(\chi^2 = \sum\limits_{i=1}^n{\frac{(O_i - E_i)^2}{E_i}}\); where \(n\) is the number of categories, \(O_i\) is the observed frequency and \(E_i\) the expected frequency of the \(i\)th category

Goodness-of-fit test

  • This is our test statistic
    • Because it’s a sum of squares (and because of its name!), we know that it follows a \(\chi^2\)-distribution
  • We need to figure out how many degrees of freedom we have
    • There are 3 categories, each one with its own frequency
    • If we fix the number of data points to 148, the frequencies within how many categories vary freely?
    • \(df = n-1\)

Goodness-of-fit test

  • Before we rely on the theoretical \(\chi^2\)-distribution, let’s simulate the sampling distribution of our calculated \(\chi^2\)-statistic under the null
# take 100,000 samples of 148 draws from
# a uniform distribution of the 3 programmes
pop <- replicate(10^5, sample(names(exp), 148, replace = T))

# define a function that calculates the chi^2 stat
chi_fun <- function(obser, expect) sum((table(obser) - expect)^2/expect)

# apply the function to each column of pop
chi_dist <- apply(pop, 2, function(x) chi_fun(x, exp))

Test of independence

  • Instead of specifying a model distribution, we can compare two (or more) observed distributions
  • Let’s say in addition to Lecture 1 (Martin’s), we also have the numbers on Lecture 2 (Milan’s)
  • Comparing the two distribution, we can assess whether there is a relationship between lecturer and programme representation or whether these variables are independent
  • \(H_0\) assumes independence – do we retain it or reject it?
  • Caveat: We are disregarding the fact that it’s mostly the same students attending both lectures and therefore the individual observations are not statistically independent
    • This makes the \(\chi^2\)-test technically inappropriate!

Test of independence

  • The \(\chi^2\)-statistic formula is the same as with goodness-of-fit test, it just gets applied to each cell, not just one row: \(\chi^2 = \sum\limits_{i = 1}^{n_{row}}\sum\limits_{j = 1}^{n_{col}}\frac{(O_{ij} - E_{ij})^2}{E_{ij}}\)
  • Same principle as before applies to calculating degrees of freedom: \(df = (rows - 1)(cols - 1) = 2\times 1 = 2\)
(sum((obs - exp)^2/exp)) # chi^2-statistic

Test of independence

  • The \(\chi^2\) test of independence showed that there is a relationship between programme representation at lectures and lecturer, \(\chi^2(2) = 9.54,\ p = .009\)
  • We can thus reject the null hypothesis of independence

Assumptions

  • We mentioned that the test of independence was not appropriate for our scenario – it violates one of the assumptions underlying the test:
    • Random sample – data are sampled from the population at random
    • Expected cell count – expected cell counts must be at least 5 in each of the cells of the contingency table
    • Independence – observations are independent of each other (no repeated measures, no correlated observations, no paired samples)

The z -test

Known \(\sigma^2\)

  • We talked about the z-test last time, even though we didn’t call it by that name
  • The z-test requires us to know the population variance \(\sigma^2\)
  • Two main uses:
    • Is the mean what we expect it to be (test of location)?
    • Is there a difference between the means of 2 groups?

Test of location

  • The ape index compares a person’s arm span to their height
    • Positive ape index means, that your arm span is larger then your height
    • 165 cm (5′5″) tall person with arm span of 167 cm has an ape index of +2
  • In general population, the mean ape index is 0 and the s is 3.34
  • Elite rock climbers tend to have a positive ape index
  • Let’s visit national rock climbing competitions and measure contestants’ ape indices

 

                Ashima Shiraishi
                156 cm tall
                Ape index +10 cm












Test of location

  • We measured the ape index (AI) of 253 elite rock climbers and found their mean AI to be +2.87 cm
  • \(H\): Mean AI of elite rock climbers is not the same as mean population AI, \(\bar{x}_{climb} \ne \mu\)
  • \(H_0\): Mean AI of elite rock climbers is 0, \(\bar{x}_{climb} = \mu\)

Test of location

  • First, we need to calculate the sample mean
  • Then we need a measure of the distance between sample mean and expected \(\mu\)
  • Taking all possible sample of n = 253 from the population of the null hypothesis (e.g., climbers are from the general population), the differences between the sample means of the individual samples and the expected \(\mu\) give us the sampling distribution of \(\bar{x} - \mu\)
  • The sampling distribution will approach normal (according to CLT)

Test of location

n <- 253; mu <- 0; sigma <- 3.34
# for 100,000 samples get sample mean - mu
diff <- replicate(10^5, mean(rnorm(n, mu, sigma)) - mu)
hist(diff, breaks = 100, xlab = expression(bar(x)-mu),
     main = expression(Sampling~distribution~under~H[0]))

Test of location

  • The SD of the sampling distribution is the SE of the difference \(\bar{x} - \mu\)
  • \(SE = \sigma /\sqrt{n}\)
# close enough!
sd(diff)
[1] 0.2101372
(se <- sigma/sqrt(n))
[1] 0.209984

Test of location

  • Scaling the sampling distribution by SE turns it into standard normal (mean = 0, SD = 1)
diff_scale <- diff/se
hist(diff_scale, breaks = 100, xlab = expression(frac(bar(x)-mu, SE)),
     main = expression(Sampling~distribution~under~H[0]))

Test of location

  • We can also divide our measured difference by SE to get the z-statistic
    • \(z = \frac{\bar{x}-\mu}{SE}\)
  • All that’s left is to find value of z in the sampling distribution and associated p-value
(z <- (mean(climb) - mu)/se)
[1] 15.41326
# empirical 2-tailed p-value
sum(abs(diff_scale) >= z)/length(diff_scale)
[1] 0
# 2-tailed p-value using std normal distribution
pnorm(z, 0, 1, lower.tail = F) * 2
[1] 1.333109e-53

Test of location

  • \(p = 1.33\times 10^{-53}\) – result is highly statistically significant so we reject \(H_0\)

Mean differences

  • Slightly modified z-test can be used for comparing 2 groups on some continuous variable, if population variances are known
  • AI is also predictive of performance in swimmers (Michael Phelps has the same arm span/height ratio as Ashima)
  • Who has a larger mean AI, climbers or swimmers?
  • \(H:\) There is a difference in mean AI between elite climbers and elite swimmers – \(\bar{x}_1 \ne \bar{x}_2\)
  • \(H_0:\) Elite climbers and elite swimmers have the same mean AI – \(\bar{x}_1 = \bar{x}_2\)

Mean differences

# get 300 elite swimmers
swim <- rnorm(300, 3.32, 1.5)

Mean differences

  • Again, the sampling distribution of the mean differences scaled by its SE is a standard normal distribution
  • z statistic is used to get p-values
  • \(SE = \sqrt{\sigma_1^2/n_1 + \sigma_2^2/n_2}\)
  • \(z = \frac{(\bar{x}_1 - \bar{x}_2) - (\mu_1 - \mu_2)}{SE}\)
  • Under \(H_0\), there is no difference between population means, so \(\mu_1 - \mu_2 = 0\)
var_climb <- 1.94^2
var_swim <- 1.5^2
(z <- (mean(climb) - mean(swim))/

pnorm(abs(z), lower.tail = F) * 2

Student’s t -test

Unknown \(\sigma^2\)

  • When doing a z-test, we were scaling the difference between expected value and observed value by SE
  • SE is calculated using population variance (or SD)
  • What is the problem?
    • We don’t know \(\sigma^2\) so we can’t calculate SE!

Estimating SE

  • We know we can estimate SE from sample variance as \(\widehat{SE} = \sqrt{\frac{s^2}{n}}\)
  • \(\widehat{SE}\) will be different in every sample – it’s a variable, while \(SE\) is a single number
  • \(\widehat{SE}\) is based on sum of squares
    • What is its distribution?
    • \(\chi^2\) with \(n - 1\) degrees of freedom

Estimating SE

n <- 263
# SE estimates from population of climbers with mu = 3 and sigma = 1.94
se_hat <- replicate(10^4, sd(rnorm(n, 3, 1.94))/sqrt(n))
mean(se_hat) # mean of the sampling dist of SE hat
[1] 0.1195214
1.94/sqrt(n) # compare to actual SE
[1] 0.1196255
hist(se_hat, breaks = 50,
     xlab = expression(widehat(italic(SE))), main = "")

Test of location – unknown \(\sigma^2\)

  • When population variance (and therefore SE) is unknown, we can’t calculate \(z = \frac{\bar{x} - \mu}{SE}\)
  • We can substitute for SE,   \(\frac{\bar{x} - \mu}{\widehat{SE}}\)
  • What is the sampling distribution of \(\bar{x} - \mu\)?
    • If \(\bar{x} - \mu\) is distributed normally and \(\widehat{SE}\) according to \(\chi^2\) with \(n - 1\) degrees of freedom, what is the distribution of \(\frac{\bar{x} - \mu}{\widehat{SE}}\)?
    • t-distribution with \(df = n-1\)
  • This is the formula for one sample t-test

One sample t -test

se_hat <- sqrt(var(climb)/ n) # estimated SE
t <- mean(climb)/se_hat # t-statistic
pt(t, n - 1, lower.tail = F) * 2 # 2-tailed p-value
[1] 5.16621e-76

Mean differences – unknown \(\sigma^2\)

  • Logic behind one-sample t-test also applies to comparing differences in means of two groups
  • Two scenarios for comparing groups
    • Observations across groups are unrelated - independent t-test
    • Observations are related (repeated measures, pairs of siblings etc.) - paired samples t-test

Paired samples t -test

  • Used in repeated measures design or when pairs of observations are matched
  • When individual data points across two group are related, independent t-test is not appropriate
    • Estimated pooled variance of the two groups is larger then the variance in differences between the matched points
  • Which is faster in a 100 m race, front crawl or butterfly stroke?
    • 20 elite female swimmers completed two individual 100 m time trials using each of the styles (order was randomised)

Assumptions

  • Both z and t-test requires the numerator (\(\bar{x} - \mu\), \((\bar{x}_1 - \bar{x}_2) - (\mu_1 - \mu_2)\), or \(\bar{x}_D - \mu\)) to be normally distributed
  • In addition, t-test assumes SD is independent of the numerator
  • Independent t-test assumes both populations have equal variances (\(\sigma^2_1 = \sigma^2_2\)) but Welch’s test doesn’t
  • t-test does not assume the data are normally distributed!
    • However, if they are, the above assumptions will be met

Take-home message

  • \(\chi^2\)-test is used to
    • assess model fit
    • see if data come from specific distribution
    • test for independence of two categorical variables
  • z-test is used when population variance is known (almost never)
  • t-test is used when population variance is unknown
    • One sample t-test is used as a test of location
    • Independent t-test is used for comparing the means of two independent groups (Welch’s test is always fine)
    • Paired samples t-test is used for repeated measures and matched pairs design

Take-home message

All follow the same procedure

  • Calculate test statistic
  • Find value of test statistic in a corresponding distribution under \(H_0\)
  • Calculate p-value that the test statistic is at least as large as the one observed, even if \(H_0\) is true

Decision errors

False positives

  • In last lecture we saw that even if there is no relationship in the population, we can still find one in the sample
  • p-values tell us how likely we are to find a given or a more extreme result in a world where the null is true
  • Sometimes, we can find a statistically significant relationship, even if there isn’t one in the population
  • This is known as the type I error (AKA false positive)

Type I error

  • Type I error is a situation of rejecting the null, when it is in fact true
    • Jury convicting the defendant if they are innocent
    • Pregnancy test showing the person taking it is pregnant even if they are not
  • There is always a chance that a significant result is due to Type I error
  • Chance of committing Type I error is known as \(\alpha\) level of the test
    • Conventionally, we want it to be 5%
    • Related but not the same as p-value

Type I error

  • If \(\alpha = .05\), we can expect 1/20 tests to reject the null hypothesis even if it’s true
# create 21 unrelated random normal variables
df_type1 <- data.frame(replicate(21, rnorm(40, 0, 1)))
# compare var 1 and each one of the 20 others with a t-test
results <- lapply(df_type1[-1, ], function(x) t.test(x, df_type1[[1]]))

Type I error

Type I error

  • Running repeated analyses on subsets of data inflates the chance of a significant result even if there is no relationship in the population
  • That is why it is bad practice to do studies like this one

False negatives

  • Earlier, we saw that an independent t-test found no difference in times on 100 m trial between front crawl and butterfly, even though there was one
  • When we fail to reject null if it really is false, we commit type II error (AKA false negative)
    • Jury acquitting the defendant even though they are guilty
    • Pregnancy test coming back negative, even if the person taking it is indeed pregnant

Type II error

  • Chance of committing Type II error is known as \(\beta\) level of the test
  • \(1 - \beta\) is then the statistical power of the test
    • Conventionally, we want it to be at least 80%
    • The larger the sample size, the higher the power
    • The larger the true population effect, the smaller the sample size needed to detect it
    • Different tests have different levels of power

Type I and II error

  • There is always a trade-off between \(\alpha\) and power
  • The more conservative a test (small chance of committing type I error), the less power it has to detect a true effect
  • Statistical power can be calculated a priori – check out this interactive app
  • Always choose appropriate tests and a large enough sample to detect effect of a given size

What p-value is not

  • We know that p-value denotes the probability that we get the result we observed or a more extreme one even if the null hypothesis is true
  • p-value less than .05 does not
    • Disprove \(H_0\)
    • Show that there is a 5% probability that \(H_0\) is true
    • Prove research hypothesis \(H_1\)
    • Calculate the probability of \(H_1\) being true
    • Calculate the probability of your decision to reject \(H_0\) being a Type I error
    • Show that repeating your experiment will give the same result 95% of the time

Quantifying confidence

Point vs interval estimates

  • Tests we’ve covered only give us estimates of single numbers representing
    • departure from expectation (\(\chi^2\))
    • differences between means (z and t)
    • mean difference between paired observations (t)
  • There is always a degree of uncertainty around these point estimates
  • It is very useful to quantify this uncertainty
  • Confidence intervals are one way of achieving this

Confidence intervals

  • Suppose we ran our study comparing ape index of climbers and swimmers on all possible samples
  • Each time, we would get a different point estimate (t) representing difference in means of the two groups
  • The true difference, however, is always the same
  • x% confidence interval is a range of values around each point estimate where x% of these ranges include the true difference
  • 95% CIs are used most frequently but the level is arbitrary

  • This is the range of values true difference can take without being significantly different from the observed one (\(-0.16\))
  • CIs that include zero indicate non-significant results

Bootstrapping

  • Ordinary CIs are, unsurprisingly, calculated using the t-statistic
    • This makes use of the fact that t is from a known distribution – \(t(df = n-1)\)
  • If the distribution of the statistic is not known, we can use resampling methods to do inferential statistics
  • Bootstrapping is a clever resampling method that’s easy to understand

Bootstrapping


The process

  1. Calculate the statistic of interest
  2. Reshuffle your data by taking a sample of equal size with replacement
  3. Re-calculate the statistic and save its value
  4. Repeat 2. and 3. many times
  5. Compare the original observed statistic to the empirical sampling distribution

Bootstrapping p -values

  • Let’s demonstrate bootstrapping on the difference in mean ape index between climbers and swimmers
# observed statistic
obs_stat <- mean(df$ape_index[df$group == 0]) -
  mean(df$ape_index[df$group == 1])
# bootstrap
boot_dist <- c()
df2 <- df
for (i in 1:1000) {
  # RANDOMISE group by resampling its values
  df2$group <- sample(df$group, replace = T)
  boot_dist <- c( # add value to boot_dist
    boot_dist,
    # calculate the statistic on the bootstrapped sample
    mean(df2$ape_index[df2$group == 0]) -
      mean(df2$ape_index[df2$group == 1]))
}

Bootstrapping p -values

  • Now we can get the p-value based on the empirical sampling distribution
sum(abs(boot_dist) >= abs(obs_stat)) / length(boot_dist)
[1] 0.255
t.test(ape_index ~ group, df)$p.value # compare to t-test
[1] 0.2808978

Bootstrapping CIs

  • Bootstrapping can be used to obtain CIs
  • The difference in procedure is that we don’t want to randomise the data, just resample it
  • In our case, that means that we won’t reshuffle group but take sample of whole rows of df
# bootstrap for CI
boot_dist <- c()
for (i in 1:1000) {
  # resample df - ENTIRE ROWS
  df2 <- df[sample(nrow(df), replace = T), ]
  boot_dist <- c( # add value to boot_dist
    boot_dist,
    # calculate the statistic on the bootstrapped sample
    mean(df2$ape_index[df2$group == 0]) -
      mean(df2$ape_index[df2$group == 1]))
}

Mean difference as linear model

  • Differences between means of two (or multiple) groups can be conceptualised as linear model
  • Simplest way is using dummy variables
    • Let’s define variable \(D\) as a having the value of 0 for climbers and 1 for swimmers
  • \(\widehat{AI} = b_0 + b_1\times D\)
  • \(\widehat{AI} = 3.24 + 0.16\times D\)
  • \(\widehat{AI}_{climb} = 3.24 + 0.16\times 0 = 3.24\)
  • \(\widehat{AI}_{swim} = 3.24 + 0.16\times 1 = 3.4\)
  • \(b_1\) is then the difference between the group means

Take-home message

  • Type I error is the false positive
    • \(\alpha\) is the probability of committing it
    • Conventional \(\alpha\) level is .05
  • Type II error is the false negative
    • \(\beta\) is its associated probability
    • We don’t want \(\beta\) to be above .2
  • Statistical power (\(\beta - 1\)) is the power to detect a true effect, if it exists
    • We want it to be at least 80%
    • Related to sample size, type of data, model fitted, and true effect size

Take-home message

  • Confidence intervals quantify the uncertainty around point estimates
  • They give us range of values the true population parameter can take and still not be significantly different from the observed statistic
  • If they contain zero (for mean differences/slopes), result is not statistically significant
  • If CI around mean of a group overlaps with mean of another group, groups are not significantly different

Take-home message

  • Bootstrapping is a simple but powerful and elegant resampling method of doing statistics
  • Relies on data alone
  • Does not make assumptions about parameter distributions
  • It can be used to derive several types of CIs

Take-home message

  • Tests of location and mean differences are linear models with a categorical predictor
  • Easiest way to include categorical predictors is using dummy variables
    • We need one fewer dummy variables than levels of categorical predictor
    • R does it by default when the predictor is a factor
  • Intercept is the predicted mean of the reference category
  • Individual b parameters related to dummy variables are differences in predicted means of reference and given comparison group
