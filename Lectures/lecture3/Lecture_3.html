Catch-up

Dispersion

  • Mode, median, and mean tell us about the central point in our data
  • They don’t tell us how spread the data are around this point
  • That is what measures of dispersion (spread) do
    • Range
    • Inter-quartile range
    • Deviation
    • Variance
    • Standard deviation

Dispersion: Range

  • Distance between smallest and largest value in sample
  • Drawback: Extremely sensitive to outliers
# range() gives minimum and maximum
range(data$age)
[1] 23 33
# get the distance
max(data$age) - min(data$age)
[1] 10

Dispersion: IQR

  • Inter-quartile range - distance between 1st and 3rd quartile
  • Drawback: Ignores half of the data
# get quartiles (quantiles with n = 4)

Dispersion: Deviation

  • Distance from every single value in the data from some convenient point
  • Mean is a convenient point
  • \(x_i - \bar{x}\), where \(x_i\) is every single data point
# minus is a vectorised operation in R
data$age - mean(data$age)
[1] -3 -1  7  0 -1 -2

Dispersion: Deviation

  • There are as many deviations as data points
  • To get a single measure of spread, how about we add them all up?
  • Problem: More data points = more points to add up
  • BIG problem: They always add up to zero

Dispersion: Variance

  • How do we get around the BIG problem (deviations adding up to 0)?
  • By taking the square of the deviations
  • The sum of these is called the Sum of Squares
  • We can get around the problem by dividing the sum of squares by N
  • This is the variance

Dispersion: Variance

  • population variance \(\sigma^2 = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^N (x_i - \mu)^2}{N}\)
  • sample variance, \(s^2 = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^N (x_i - \bar{x})^2}{N - 1}\)
    • reason for the \(N - 1\) is quite technical (see Bessel’s correction)
# deviations
(dev <- data$age - mean(data$age))
[1] -3 -1  7  0 -1 -2
# sums of squares
(ss <- sum(dev^2))
[1] 64
# variance
ss / (length(data$age) - 1)
[1] 12.8
# in-built function
var(data$age)
[1] 12.8

Dispersion: Standard deviation

  • Variance is a good measure of dispersion and is widely used
  • One minor inconvenience – measured in squared units
    • if age is measured in years, \(s^2_{age}\) is expressed in years2, whatever those are
  • Taking the square root of variance gives us a measure of spread in the original units
  • This is the standard deviation
    • \(\sigma\) for population
    • \(s\) (or \(SD\)) for sample

Dispersion: Standard deviation


\[s = \sqrt{s^2} = \sqrt{\frac{\sum_{i=1}^N (x_i - \bar{x})^2}{N - 1}}\]

Functions

  • Informally, function a process that takes some input and turns it into a unique output
  • Individual inputs are called parameters
  • The process itself is the definition of a function
  • \(f(x) = x^2\)

  • \(f(1) = 1\)

Distribution

  • Numerically speaking, the number of observations per each value of a variable
  • Tells us which values occur more often and which less often
  • Graphically speaking, it is the shape formed by the bars of a bar chart/histogram
  • There are many well-described distributions with known properties, some crucial, others more esoteric

Rolling a die

The uniform distribution

  • Given that we had a fair 6-sided die, we know that the probability of any result is 1/6
  • The height of all the bars should be the same – uniform distribution
  • The observed bars are not all the same because of a reltively small finite sample and statistical fluctuations in sampling

Fluctuations

  • In smaller samples, the fluctuations are more pronounced, in large samples, they become negligible

Fluctuations

    • Empirical (observed) distribution will always differ form theoretical (expected) distribution
    • As sample size gets bigger, the observed values will approach the expected values – Law of Large Numbers

Area below the normal curve

  • No matter the particular shape of the given normal distribution, the proportions with respect to SD are the same
    • ∼68.2% of the are below the curve is within ±1 SD from the mean
    • ∼95.4% of the are below the curve is within ±2 SD from the mean
    • ∼99.7% of the are below the curve is within ±3 SD from the mean
  • We can calculate the proportion of the area with respect to any two points

Area below the normal curve

  • Say we want to know the number of SDs from the mean beyond which lie the outer 5% of the distribution
  • Since the normal curve is symmetrical, we need to know the cut-off point for the bottom 2.5%

qnorm(p = .025, mean = 0, sd = 1)
[1] -1.959964

Area below the normal curve

  • Tails of the normal distribution past the ±1.96 SD from the mean account for 5% of the data
  • That means that the probability of randomly drawing a value from the normal distribution that is smaller than or equal to −1.96 OR bigger than or equal to 1.96 SD from the mean is 5%
  • Other useful cut-off points:
qnorm(p = .005, mean = 0, sd = 1, lower.tail = F) # 1% of data is +-
[1] 2.575829

qnorm(p = .0005, mean = 0, sd = 1, lower.tail = F) # .1% is +-
[1] 3.290527

Standard normal distribution

  • Normal distribution with mean = 0 and SD = 1 is the standard normal distribution
  • Convenient – can substitute “±x SD from the mean” with “±x”, e.g.:
    • 68.2% of the standard normal distribution has values between −1 and 1
    • Most extreme 5% of the distribution lie outwith the ±1.96 cut-off point

Standard (z -) scores

  • Variable can be standardised (AKA z-transformed) by subtracting its mean and dividing by its SD
mark_z <- with(data, (mark - mean(mark)) / sd(mark))
hist(mark_z, main = "")

# the scale() function does the same
mark_z <- scale(data$mark)
In other words, we’re centering data around 0 and scaling by its SD

Other distributions

  • There are many well-known distributions and distribution families specified by other parameters
  • Similarly to the normal distribution, we can calculate the probability associated with a given portion of the area below their respective PDFs
  • Important distributions we will encounter:
    • Student’s t-distribution – for small samples and unknown population \(\sigma\)
    • \(\chi^2\)-distribution – [Chi-squared], sums of squares of random normal variables
    • F-distribution – ratio of two \(\chi^2\) variables

Departures from normality

Three ways to non-normality

  • To recap, the normal distribution is:
    • continuous unbounded
    • unimodal – has only one “peak”
    • symmetrical – given unimodality, this means that mode = median = mean
    • specifically bell-shaped – characteristic proportions (68.2% between ±1 SD from the mean, etc)
  • There are 3 ways in which the distribution of a continuous variable can depart from the normal distribution
    • Multimodality
    • Skew
    • Kurtosis

Multimodality

  • Easy to spot – does the distribution have more than one peak?

Skew

  • Skewed distributions are not symmetrical
  • That means that the statement mode = median = mean is not true
  • Positive skew means that the distribution has a long tail towards larger numbers (AKA right skew)
  • Negative (AKA left) skew means that the longer tail spreads towards smaller/negative numbers
  • Normal distribution has zero skew

Skew

  • All three distributions have mean = 0 and SD = 1

Kurtosis

  • Measure of how flat or pointy a distribution is
  • Kurtosis is independent of SD
  • Normal distribution has kurtosis = 3
  • Flat (AKA platykurtic or heavy-tailed) distributions have smaller kurtosis – their tails include more data than the tails of a normal distribution
  • Pointy (AKA leptokurtic or thin-tailed) distributions have larger kurtosis – their tails include less data than the tails of a normal distribution

Kurtosis

  • All three distributions have mean = 0 and SD = 1

Kurtosis vs SD

  • Normal distributions with small SDs may look pointy and those with large SDs may look flat but that does not mean that they don’t have normal kurtosis
  • Kurtosis is about the relationships between SD and the proportion of the area below the PDF!

Q-Q plot

Going meta

Sampling from distributions

  • Collecting data on some variable amounts to randomly* sampling from the distribution that underlies the variable
  • Often, in the case of psychological variables caused by the interplay of many factors with small effects, the underlying distribution is assumed to be normal
  • Other variables might come from other distributions
    • Reaction times are often log-normally distributed
    • Number of people who die from being kicked by a horse per year is Poisson-distributed
    • Passes/fails on an exam are binomially distributed…



* Yeah… sure…

Sampling distribution

  • If we took all possible samples of a given size (say N = 50) from the population and each time calculated the sample mean, the means would have their own distribution
  • This is the sampling distribution of the mean
    • Approximately normal
    • Centered around the true population mean
  • Every parameter has its own sampling distribution

Standard Error

Central Limit theorem

  • We mentioned that the sampling distribution of the mean is approximately normal
  • This is true even if the population from which we sample is not normally distributed
  • This is the Central Limit Theorem
